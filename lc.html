The Life Cycle of an Oyster

This image is from http://www.pangeashellfish.com.

Fertilization:
In the spring when the weather becomes warmer and the salinity of the water changes, adult oysters, typically above the age of 1 year, begin to develop gonads which will become one type of gamete (eggs or sperm). This usually takes approximately 2 months. Once this process is done, the oysters begin to release their gonads and spawning begins. When one oyster unleashes its gonad, it stimulates other oysters near by to absorb some of the sperm, and oysters who sense the sperm will in turn let out their own gonads. When the eggs and sperm meet in the water, fertilization occurs and the eggs flow downstream, away from their parents.

Larvae Growth:
After the eggs are fertilized, they divide and grow until they enter the larvae stage. They swim within the water stream for 2 weeks and feed on phytoplankton. After these weeks, the larvae travel to the bottom of the river and begin to develop a body part called a foot that they use to latch onto a hard surface, typically an oyster shell. When an appropiate place is found, the larvae discharge a sticky substance like glue to secure themselves to the shell or other hard surface. The larvae then continue to mature and enter the spat stage.

Spat To Adult:
Once the oyster larvae have matured into the spat stage, they begin to grow their shells by taking in calcium carbonate from the water surrounding them. By the end of the first year of an oyster's life, it will become an adolescent, and when it has reached the age of 3 years old, the oyster will be an adult. Once the oysters have reached this stage, the whole cycle begins again.

Words to Know:
Veliger- the final larve stage of certain mollusks, having two ciliated (tiny hair-like projections) flaps for swimming and feeding.
Pediveliger- a stage in the life cycle of a veliger that is able to crawl using its foot.
Salinity- the measure of all the salts dissolved in water.
Gonad- an organ that produces gametes.

To learn more about the lifecycle of the oyster, you can visit other resources such as the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science's Horn Point Oyster Hatchery Webpage.
